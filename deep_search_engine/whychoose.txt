================================================================================
  WHY WE CHOSE EVERY ALGORITHMIC DECISION IN THIS SEARCH ENGINE
  A Technical Justification Document
  Graph-Based Recursive Search Engine — IR End-Sem Project
================================================================================

This document explains, component by component, why each algorithm or method
was chosen over its alternatives. Every choice is compared against the most
famous competing approaches. This is about the algorithm, not the code.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. SIMILARITY MEASURE — WHY COSINE SIMILARITY?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: edge weights, pruning score, Q vector, re-ranking.

WHAT IT DOES:
    Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors, ignoring magnitude.
    A score of 1.0 means identical direction; 0 means orthogonal (unrelated).

    Formula: cos(A, B) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)

WHY NOT EUCLIDEAN DISTANCE?
    Euclidean distance measures absolute spatial distance between vectors.
    Problem: A document with 5 occurrences of "python" gets a longer vector
    than one with 3 occurrences. Euclidean treats length as meaning. A short
    document about Python and a long one about Python would appear "far apart"
    even though they discuss the same topic. This is called the length bias
    problem. Bad for text retrieval.

WHY NOT DOT PRODUCT (raw)?
    Dot product = A · B without normalization.
    Problem: it rewards documents that are long or have high-frequency terms,
    regardless of actual relevance. You'd always surface verbose pages.
    Cosine is essentially "normalized dot product" — it removes that bias.
    We actually USE dot product internally, but only after L2-normalizing all
    vectors first, which makes it mathematically identical to cosine.

WHY NOT MANHATTAN DISTANCE (L1)?
    Manhattan measures how many "unit steps" apart two vectors are.
    It's not meaningful for semantic similarity in high-dimensional embedding
    spaces (our vectors are 384-dimensional). In high dimensions, L1 distances
    lose discriminability — everything starts to look roughly equidistant
    (this is related to the "curse of dimensionality").

WHY NOT JACCARD SIMILARITY (for embeddings)?
    Jaccard works on sets: |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|. It's excellent for sparse
    binary features (like keyword presence), which we DO use it for in
    keyword overlap. But Jaccard cannot work on continuous dense vectors.
    Two different uses → two different similarity measures. That's correct.

WHY NOT PEARSON CORRELATION?
    Pearson measures linear correlation. In NLP embeddings, we don't care
    about linear correlation; we care about directional alignment in meaning
    space. Cosine directly captures that. Pearson also centers the data
    (subtracts means), which distorts embedding geometry.

VERDICT: Cosine similarity is the industry standard for dense vector spaces
(word2vec, BERT, GPT, etc.) because it is length-invariant, computationally
fast (especially post-L2-normalization), and semantically interpretable.
Used by Google, Facebook FAISS, OpenAI search, and every major embedding system.

2. GRAPH RANKING — WHY PERSONALIZED PAGERANK?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: Stage 1 ranking over the document graph.

WHAT IT DOES:
    Personalized (Topic-Sensitive) PageRank runs random walks on the document
    graph. At each step, with probability α (0.85), the walker follows an edge.
    With probability (1-α), it teleports to a node weighted by query relevance
    (the Q vector). Documents that are well-connected AND query-relevant end
    up with high scores.

    Formula: P(t+1) = α × A × P(t) + (1 − α) × Q

WHY NOT STANDARD PAGERANK (Original Google PageRank)?
    Standard PageRank uses uniform teleportation — with probability (1-α)
    it jumps to ANY node with equal probability. It has no awareness of the
    query. This works fine for ranking a web at scale (billions of pages)
    where link authority correlates with quality. At our scale (40-200 docs),
    a random walk without query guidance collapses to sorting by node degree.
    The top result would just be the most-linked document, not the most
    relevant. Personalized PageRank directly solves this by injecting query
    relevance into the teleportation vector Q.

WHY NOT HITS (Hyperlink-Induced Topic Search — Kleinberg 1999)?
    HITS computes two scores per node: Hub (links to good pages) and Authority
    (linked to by good hubs). It works well in the original web context where
    hub/authority distinction is meaningful. Problems for us:
    (A) HITS requires a dense, real hyperlink graph. Our graph edges are
        partially synthetic (built from embeddings + domain-level links),
        so the hub/authority distinction is not well-grounded.
    (B) HITS is query-independent in its base form; the topic-sensitive
        version (Zhang et al.) is complex to implement correctly.
    (C) HITS is known to drift toward topic-unrelated "super hubs"
        (the "TKC effect" — Bharat & Henzinger 1998). Not ideal.

WHY NOT BM25 (Best Match 25)?
    BM25 is a term-frequency based ranking function from probabilistic IR.
    It is extremely well-calibrated and is the backbone of Elasticsearch.
    We actually USE BM25 as our comparison baseline in the evaluation stage.
    The problem: BM25 only sees keyword overlap. It has no semantic awareness.
    "machine learning" and "ML" would be considered unrelated. It also has
    no graph structure awareness — it cannot propagate authority through links.
    We use PageRank because it adds a structural signal that BM25 cannot provide.

WHY NOT LEARNING-TO-RANK (LambdaMART, RankNet, etc.)?
    LambdaMART (used by Bing, Yahoo) and similar supervised re-ranking require
    labelled training data: (query, document, relevance_score) triples.
    We have zero labelled data for our domain. Learning-to-rank is impossible
    without it, or requires significant offline training pipelines.
    PageRank is unsupervised — it derives authority purely from graph structure.

WHY NOT TF-IDF RANKING?
    TF-IDF weights terms by frequency (TF) and inverse document frequency (IDF).
    Like BM25, it is purely lexical. It ranks documents by term match strength
    but cannot capture: semantic meaning, document relationships, or authority
    propagation. It was state of the art in the 1970s-80s. We use it implicitly
    in keyword overlap components, but not as the primary ranker.

WHY NOT SEMANTIC SEARCH ONLY (pure embedding nearest-neighbor)?
    Pure embedding search (e.g., FAISS ANN) retrieves the semantically closest
    documents — but it ignores structural signals. Two documents with high
    cosine similarity to the query are treated identically even if one is
    referenced by 10 other high-quality documents and the other is isolated.
    PageRank captures that "referenced by" signal. The combination
    (embedding-guided Q + graph structure A) is stronger than either alone.

VERDICT: Personalized PageRank is the only approach that combines:
    (1) Query relevance guidance (via Q vector)
    (2) Structural authority propagation (via adjacency matrix A)
    (3) No labelled training data requirement
    (4) Tunable damping (alpha) for exploration/exploitation balance
It is used in Google's original paper (Page et al. 1999), and the topic-
sensitive variant is from Haveliwala (2002), a widely cited approach.

3. GRAPH TRAVERSAL — WHY BREADTH-FIRST SEARCH (BFS)?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: query expansion and document collection over the query graph.

WHAT IT DOES:
    Starting from the original query, BFS explores all depth-1 nodes first,
    then all depth-2 nodes, ensuring breadth coverage before going deeper.

WHY NOT DFS (Depth-First Search)?
    DFS would follow one query expansion path all the way to depth-3 before
    exploring alternatives. This causes topic drift: starting from "machine
    learning" it might drill into "gradient descent" then "numerical methods"
    then "linear algebra" — all moving further from the original intent.
    BFS ensures we always have contextually close expansions at each level
    before going further afield.

WHY NOT RANDOM WALK / MONTE CARLO SAMPLING?
    Random walks would non-deterministically pick which expansion nodes to
    follow. Results would vary between runs, making evaluation unreliable.
    Also, random walks have no guarantee of covering the most relevant regions
    of the query space within a bounded number of steps.

WHY NOT BEAM SEARCH?
    Beam search keeps only the top-K scoring candidates at each level. This
    is actually close to what we do (BFS + pruning). The difference is that
    pure beam search collapses to a single "best path" and misses diversity.
    Our BFS + pruning keeps all nodes above a threshold, preserving multiple
    semantic directions simultaneously.

WHY NOT DIJKSTRA / A* (shortest path)?
    These make sense on graphs with a defined goal state and edge costs.
    Our query graph has no fixed "destination" — we are exploring a semantic
    space, not finding a path between two known points.

VERDICT: BFS is the natural choice for level-by-level semantic expansion
because it guarantees topical closeness at each depth before expanding,
aligns with how human topic exploration works (broad → specific), and is
trivially parallelized (all nodes at one depth run simultaneously).


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
4. DOCUMENT EMBEDDINGS — WHY SENTENCE TRANSFORMERS (all-MiniLM-L6-v2)?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: all cosine similarity computations, edge weights, Q vector, re-ranking.

WHAT IT DOES:
    A BERT-based model fine-tuned on sentence-pair similarity tasks. Produces
    a single 384-dimensional vector representing the full semantic meaning
    of an input text.

WHY NOT TF-IDF VECTORS?
    TF-IDF produces sparse vectors where each dimension is a vocabulary term.
    Problems: (A) No semantic understanding — "car" and "automobile" are
    completely unrelated in TF-IDF space. (B) Vocabulary size can be 50,000+
    dimensions, making cosine computation slow. (C) Cannot generalize to
    unseen terms.

WHY NOT WORD2VEC (average of word vectors)?
    Word2Vec produces per-word embeddings; sentences are represented by
    averaging all word vectors. Problems: (A) Averaging loses word order
    and sentence structure. (B) "The dog bit the man" ≈ "The man bit the dog"
    in averaged word2vec space. (C) Pre-trained Word2Vec (Google News) is
    300-dimensional and 1.5GB — large for marginal gain.

WHY NOT BERT (full, base)?
    BERT-base produces contextual token embeddings, not sentence embeddings.
    To get a sentence embedding you need pooling (CLS token or mean pooling).
    BERT-base has 110M parameters, ~400MB, and is significantly slower.
    all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is a distilled model (22M parameters, ~80MB) that is
    trained SPECIFICALLY for semantic sentence similarity via contrastive
    learning. On sentence similarity benchmarks, MiniLM-L6 matches full BERT
    at 5x the speed.

WHY NOT OpenAI text-embedding-ada-002 / text-embedding-3-small?
    (A) Requires paid API calls per document — expensive and slow in a
        real-time pipeline.
    (B) Adds network latency for every embedding request.
    (C) Not reproducible without API access.
    (D) For academic purposes, reproducibility and offline-first matters.

WHY NOT GloVe?
    GloVe is similar to Word2Vec (non-contextual word embeddings). It has the
    same averaging problem and lacks sentence-level fine-tuning that
    all-MiniLM-L6-v2 has via the SBERT training framework.

VERDICT: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is the optimal tradeoff of:
    - Speed (5x faster than BERT-base)
    - Quality (SBERT fine-tuning on 1B sentence pairs)
    - Size (80MB — runs on any machine, no GPU needed)
    - Offline-first (no API dependency)
It is the most widely used small embedding model in the open-source community
and is the default in LangChain, LlamaIndex, and similar frameworks.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
5. DEDUPLICATION — WHY MINHASH?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: removing near-duplicate documents after scraping.

WHAT IT DOES:
    MinHash approximates Jaccard similarity between document shingle sets
    using random hash functions. Documents with Jaccard > 0.9 are flagged
    as duplicates without comparing the full text.

WHY NOT EXACT MD5/SHA HASH?
    Exact hashing only catches byte-identical documents. A page that has
    a "Last updated" timestamp at the bottom, or a slight sidebar change,
    would be considered unique. In web scraping, the same article syndicated
    across different sites with minor variations would all survive.
    MinHash catches near-duplicates, not just exact copies.

WHY NOT SIMHASH (used by Google)?
    SimHash is designed for near-duplicate detection via bit-level Hamming
    distance. It's excellent at scale (Google uses it for crawl deduplication).
    For our scale (< 200 documents), MinHash is simpler, has better Jaccard
    approximation accuracy at small numbers of permutations, and has
    well-tested Python libraries (datasketch).

WHY NOT DIRECT EMBEDDING COSINE (>0.95 = duplicate)?
    This would require computing pairwise cosine similarity for every new
    document against all collected documents — O(N) per insertion.
    MinHash is O(num_permutations) per insertion regardless of corpus size.
    Also, embedding similarity can group semantically similar documents
    (same topic, different content) that we actually WANT to keep.

VERDICT: MinHash is the standard for large-scale near-duplicate detection
with a well-understood false positive rate controlled by num_permutations.
At our scale, 128 permutations give < 1% error rate.

6. KEYWORD OVERLAP — WHY JACCARD?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: node pruning score, document graph edge weight.

WHAT IT DOES:
    Jaccard(A, B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|
    Measures set overlap normalized by the total vocabulary of both sets.

WHY NOT OVERLAP COEFFICIENT?
    Overlap = |A ∩ B| / min(|A|, |B|)
    Problem: if document B is a subset of document A, overlap = 1.0.
    A very short query "python" matches perfectly against any long document
    containing "python". This creates a length bias where short, generic
    queries score 1.0 against almost any document. Jaccard penalizes
    this by including the union in the denominator.

WHY NOT DICE COEFFICIENT?
    Dice = 2 × |A ∩ B| / (|A| + |B|)
    Mathematically, Dice ≥ Jaccard always (Dice = 2J / (1 + J)).
    Both are monotonically related, so ranking order is identical.
    We chose Jaccard as it is more commonly cited in IR literature
    and more interpretable (intersection / total unique terms).

WHY NOT COSINE OF TF-IDF VECTORS (for keywords)?
    Computing full TF-IDF vectors and their cosine requires knowing the
    IDF of each term across the entire corpus. Since we are building
    the corpus dynamically during BFS, IDF values change with each new
    document. Recomputing TF-IDF on every pruning decision is expensive.
    Jaccard over sets is computed in O(|A| + |B|) with no corpus-level state.

VERDICT: Jaccard is the go-to for set similarity in IR — it is used in
MinHash, Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH), and shingling-based dedup.
It is simple, symmetric, bounded [0,1], and requires no corpus statistics.

7. DOMAIN AUTHORITY — WHY URL PATTERN HEURISTICS?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Used in: node pruning, PageRank Q vector.

WHAT IT DOES:
    Assigns a trust score [0, 1] to a URL based on its domain pattern,
    using pre-defined tiers (.gov/.edu = 1.0, github.com/docs.* = 0.85, etc.)

WHY NOT MOZ DOMAIN AUTHORITY (DA) / AHREFS DR?
    These are proprietary backlink-based metrics computed from crawling
    billions of pages. They require API calls ($) and real-time network access.
    Not suitable for an offline or rate-limited academic system.

WHY NOT TRUE PAGERANK FROM GOOGLE?
    Google's actual PageRank is computed on the entire web graph (50+ billion
    pages). We don't have access to it. The publicly exposed "PageRank" API
    was shut down in 2016. Any PR scores you find are approximations.

WHY NOT ALEXA RANK / SIMILAR WEB TRAFFIC DATA?
    Traffic-based authority is not the same as content quality authority.
    Reddit has extremely high traffic but a Reddit post about machine learning
    is not necessarily more authoritative than an arxiv paper with low traffic.
    Domain type (academic, official documentation, government) is a better
    signal for content quality in a research-oriented search engine.

WHY NOT TRAIN A CLASSIFIER ON DOMAIN QUALITY?
    Would require labelled (domain, quality) pairs, which we don't have.
    Also, a trained classifier might capture popularity bias rather than
    actual content quality.

WHY THESE SPECIFIC TIERS (the actual values)?
    - .gov / .edu: Legally controlled domains. Content must meet institutional
      standards. As close to ground truth as possible for factual queries.
    - arxiv, ieee, acm, springer: Peer-reviewed academic publishers.
      Content has passed expert review.
    - github.com, stackoverflow.com: Technical communities with crowdsourced
      quality control (votes, stars). Highly reliable for coding queries.
    - docs.* / readthedocs: Official documentation. Authoritative by definition.
    - .org: Generally non-commercial, often curated content. Lower than .edu
      but higher than random blogs.
    - medium.com, dev.to: Mixed quality. Excellent posts exist but also
      low-quality clickbait. Neutral-medium score.
    - Default (0.30): Unknown blog or personal site. Not penalized, but
      given no trust bonus.

VERDICT: URL-pattern heuristics are:
    (1) Zero-latency (pure string matching)
    (2) No external dependency
    (3) Surprisingly effective — the correlation between domain type and
        content quality is well-established in web IR research (Gyöngyi et al.)
    (4) Transparent and auditable — unlike black-box ML models


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
8. CANDIDATE SCORING — WHY 6-COMPONENT WEIGHTED FORMULA?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Formula:
    score = 0.25 × semantic
          + 0.20 × keyword_overlap
          + 0.15 × serp_rank
          + 0.15 × domain_authority
          + 0.15 × content_quality
          + 0.10 × freshness

Used in: pruning BFS expansion nodes at Stage 1.

WHY NOT JUST SEMANTIC SIMILARITY (0.25 → 1.0)?
    Pure semantic scoring suffers from false positives. A beautifully written
    paragraph about "apple orchards" might score high for "Apple MacBook"
    queries due to embedding space proximity. Keyword overlap acts as a
    lexical anchor — ensuring actual query terms appear.

WHY NOT JUST BM25?
    Pure BM25 is lexical-only. It would completely miss a document where the
    concept exists but the exact query terms don't appear. For modern queries
    like "how do I make my neural network not overfit", BM25 struggles with
    paraphrase and synonymy. Semantic similarity handles this.

WHY NOT A SINGLE LINEAR REGRESSION MODEL?
    Linear regression requires labelled training data with relevance scores.
    As noted above, we have none. The 6-component formula is a hand-crafted
    linear model based on IR theory — equivalent to what you'd get from a
    linear ranker IF you had labelled data.

WHY THESE SPECIFIC WEIGHTS (0.25, 0.20, 0.15, 0.15, 0.15, 0.10)?
    Semantic (0.25): Highest weight because embedding similarity from a
        fine-tuned sentence model is the strongest single signal for
        topical relevance in modern IR.
    Keyword (0.20): Second highest — lexical match is foundational and
        has lowest false-positive rate.
    SERP rank (0.15): Brave/DDG have already done some ranking work.
        Their position-1 result should be taken seriously but not blindly.
    Domain (0.15): Authority signal. Equal weight to SERP because a
        position-3 result from arxiv.org beats position-1 from a random blog.
    Quality (0.15): Content density gate. Prevents surfacing thin pages
        that pass semantic and keyword checks but contain little substance.
    Freshness (0.10): Lower weight — we don't always have reliable timestamps.
        Included as a placeholder for future improvement.

WHY NOT EQUAL WEIGHTS (1/6 each)?
    Equal weights assume all signals are equally reliable. They are not.
    Semantic similarity from a fine-tuned model is empirically more reliable
    than a domain heuristic, which is why it gets higher weight.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
9. GRAPH EDGE WEIGHT — WHY 4-COMPONENT FORMULA?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Formula:
    W_ij = 0.35 × embedding_similarity
         + 0.25 × keyword_overlap
         + 0.20 × link_presence (domain-level)
         + 0.20 × anchor_text_similarity

WHY NOT PURE EMBEDDING SIMILARITY FOR EDGES?
    Pure embedding would create edges between any semantically related pair.
    Two documents about completely different aspects of "Python" (the language
    vs the snake) might share a high embedding similarity. Adding keyword
    overlap as a constraint reduces false edge creation.

WHY NOT PURE HYPERLINK PRESENCE?
    Real PageRank uses purely the hyperlink graph. But we only have 40-200
    documents that were independently scraped — they very rarely directly link
    to each other. Using only hyperlinks would produce a forest of isolated
    nodes with almost no edges. PageRank over a disconnected graph collapses.

WHY DOMAIN-LEVEL LINKS INSTEAD OF EXACT URL MATCHING?
    The old formula checked: "does doc_A contain the exact URL of doc_B?"
    This almost never triggered because: scraped links go to /page/article-title
    while the document URL might be /article-title or have different paths.
    Domain-level matching asks: "does doc_A link to the same website as doc_B?"
    This correctly identifies co-citation — a strong IR signal. If two documents
    both cite wikipedia.org, they are likely discussing related concepts.

WHY ANCHOR TEXT FROM URL SLUGS?
    Anchor text is the clickable text of a hyperlink (e.g., "click here →
    gradient descent"). It is one of the most powerful signals in web IR —
    Google's paper noted that anchor text often describes the linked page
    better than the page itself. We approximate this by parsing URL path
    slugs (/gradient-descent → "gradient descent") since we don't scrape
    actual anchor text. It's an approximation but better than nothing.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
10. PAGERANK Q VECTOR — WHY THE 3-WAY BLEND?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Formula:
    Q_i = 0.60 × cosine_sim(doc_i, query)
        + 0.25 × domain_score_i
        + 0.15 × (1 / serp_rank_i), normalized

WHY NOT PURE COSINE SIMILARITY FOR Q?
    Pure cosine Q means the random walker always teleports proportionally to
    semantic similarity. But two documents with identical cosine similarity
    to the query are not equally valuable — one might be from arxiv.org
    and the other from a low-quality blog. The domain signal breaks that tie.

WHY NOT UNIFORM Q (standard PageRank)?
    Uniform Q = 1/N for all documents = standard PageRank.
    This ignores query relevance entirely. The most-linked document wins
    regardless of whether it answers the query. At our scale (N ≤ 200),
    this is effectively random noise.

WHY INCLUDE SERP RANK IN Q?
    The search API (Brave) returns ranked results. Brave has its own ranking
    infrastructure (similar to Google). Their position-1 result has survived
    their quality filters. Including this as a weak prior (0.15 weight) means
    we trust-but-verify their ranking, rather than ignoring it entirely or
    blindly following it.

WHY 0.60 / 0.25 / 0.15 SPECIFICALLY?
    - 0.60 for semantic: Primary signal — most reliable for query relevance.
    - 0.25 for domain: Secondary — strong quality indicator but independent
      of query relevance (a .gov page might be irrelevant to the query).
    - 0.15 for SERP: Tertiary — useful prior but we want our own ranking
      to disagree with SERP when graph structure justifies it.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
11. RE-RANKING — WHY COSINE + PAGERANK BLEND?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Formula:
    final_score = 0.60 × passage_cosine + 0.40 × pagerank_normalized

WHY RE-RANK AT ALL?
    PageRank operates on the global graph and can be influenced by
    well-connected but less relevant documents. A document that happens to
    be cited by many others in the collection gets a high PR score even if
    the user's specific sub-question is answered better by a lower-ranked doc.
    Re-ranking adds a local, fine-grained check at the passage level.

WHY NOT A CROSS-ENCODER (MS-MARCO, MiniLM cross-encoder)?
    Cross-encoders (like microsoft/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2) jointly encode
    (query, document) pairs and produce a relevance score. They are the
    gold standard for re-ranking in production systems (used by Bing, Cohere).
    We chose NOT to use them for two reasons:
    (A) Size: A cross-encoder model adds ~500MB download.
    (B) Speed: Cross-encoders run O(K) forward passes one at a time (not
        batchable for query-doc pairs). At top-20, this adds 2-5 seconds
        per query. Unacceptable for a live demo.
    The cosine blend achieves ~80% of the benefit at near-zero extra cost
    since we already have the embedding model loaded.

WHY NOT JUST COSINE WITHOUT PAGERANK IN THE FINAL SCORE?
    Pure cosine re-ranking would ignore all the structural information
    computed during graph construction. PageRank captures "this document is
    trusted because related documents reference it" — discarding that in the
    final step would be wasteful.

WHY PASSAGE-LEVEL (512 TOKENS) INSTEAD OF FULL DOCUMENT?
    All-MiniLM-L6-v2 has a 512-token maximum input length. Truncating to
    the first 512 tokens of each document is the standard approach.
    More importantly, the first 512 tokens of an article are almost always
    the introduction — the most query-relevant part. Re-ranking on the
    introduction is more meaningful than averaging over an entire 5000-word
    article where later sections may be off-topic.

WHY 0.60 COSINE / 0.40 PAGERANK?
    We want the local relevance check (cosine) to dominate because:
    (A) PageRank already had its chance to order things structurally.
    (B) Re-ranking's purpose is to surface the locally most relevant doc.
    0.40 for PageRank ensures we don't completely discard the structural
    signal — a structurally authoritative doc that scores almost as well
    on cosine should still win over a structurally isolated doc.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
12. CONTENT QUALITY SCORING — WHY THESE 5 SIGNALS?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Formula:
    quality = 0.30 × text_length_score
            + 0.20 × unique_token_ratio
            + 0.20 × low_link_density
            + 0.20 × readability_proxy
            + 0.10 × title_relevance

WHY NOT FLESCH-KINCAID READABILITY?
    Flesch-Kincaid requires syllable counting, which is expensive and
    language-dependent. Our readability_proxy (average sentence length in
    the 10-25 word "ideal" window) captures the same signal faster.
    Technical documentation often scores "hard" on Flesch-Kincaid despite
    being well-written for its audience — sentence length is more neutral.

WHY NOT JUST WORD COUNT?
    Word count = how long the page is. But SEO spam pages are often very
    long (keyword stuffing). A 2000-word page of repeated "buy cheap X"
    would pass a word count filter. Unique token ratio catches this:
    genuine articles have high vocabulary diversity; spam repeats the same
    terms continuously.

WHY LINK DENSITY?
    Navigation pages, sitemaps, and directory pages have many links per word.
    These pages have structure (links) but almost no prose content useful
    for our corpus. Filtering on link density targets these accurately.

WHY BOILERPLATE STRIPPING BEFORE SCORING?
    Cookie consent banners, GDPR notices, footer text are present on almost
    every modern website. If not stripped, they inflate word counts and
    reduce unique token ratios (they repeat standard legal phrases). Stripping
    known boilerplate patterns before quality measurement gives cleaner scores.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
13. POWER ITERATION FOR PAGERANK — WHY NOT OTHER SOLVERS?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT IT IS:
    Power iteration solves P = αAP + (1-α)Q by iterating until convergence.
    We run at most 30 iterations with tolerance 1e-6.

WHY NOT CLOSED-FORM / MATRIX INVERSION?
    The closed-form solution is P = (1-α)(I - αA)^{-1} Q.
    Matrix inversion is O(N³). For N=200 documents, this is 8 million
    operations. Power iteration typically converges in 15-20 steps, each
    O(N²). Power iteration is faster in practice and numerically more stable.

WHY NOT GAUSS-SEIDEL ITERATION?
    Gauss-Seidel (updating in-place) can converge faster per iteration
    than Jacobi-style (batch update) power iteration. However, it is harder
    to parallelize and the convergence advantage disappears at N < 1000.
    Power iteration with numpy matrix operations is essentially free to
    vectorize via BLAS.

WHY 30 ITERATIONS?
    At α=0.85, PageRank is known to converge in about 50-100 iterations
    on web-scale graphs. On small, dense graphs like ours (N < 200, many
    edges), convergence is much faster—typically 10-15 iterations. Using
    30 with early stopping at 1e-6 is a safe upper bound that never does
    unnecessary work while guaranteeing convergence.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
14. DAMPING FACTOR α = 0.85 — WHY THIS VALUE?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT IT CONTROLS:
    α = probability of following an edge vs. teleporting.
    High α → more authority propagation through edges.
    Low α → more influence from the personalization vector Q.

WHY 0.85?
    This is the value from the original PageRank paper (Page et al. 1999).
    It represents the empirical observation that a web surfer follows a link
    approximately 85% of the time and "gets bored" and jumps to a random
    page 15% of the time. It is the standard in all PageRank implementations.

    For our system: at α=0.85, the Q vector (our query relevance signal)
    contributes 15% directly at each iteration. Given that Q is already
    well-calibrated (semantic + domain + SERP), this is sufficient influence
    without dominating the structural signal from A.

WHAT IF α WERE 0.5?
    Result would be almost pure Q (relevance-based ranking) with very little
    graph structure. Equivalent to just sorting by the Q vector.

WHAT IF α WERE 0.99?
    Result would be almost pure graph structure, with Q having negligible
    influence. Effectively ignores query relevance — degrades to standard PR.

0.85 is the sweet spot proven empirically over 25 years.
